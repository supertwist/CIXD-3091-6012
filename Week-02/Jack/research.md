
Below is a comparison of five common hypoallergenic dog breeds. To provide a clear overview, I have detailed their general characteristics, grooming requirements, and the specific reasons they are considered allergy-friendly.

Note: Scientific consensus emphasizes that no dog is 100% hypoallergenic. All dogs produce allergenic proteins in their saliva, urine, and dander. Breeds labeled as "hypoallergenic" typically have hair rather than fur, or do not shed, which helps trap dander against the skin instead of releasing it into the home environment.

Summary of Key Findings
The "Myth" of Hypoallergenic: Major health organizations like the AAAAI state that "hypoallergenic" is often a marketing term. Studies comparing dust samples from homes with hypoallergenic vs. regular dogs found no significant difference in environmental allergen levels.

Hair vs. Fur: Most of these breeds are successful for allergy sufferers because they have hair (which grows continuously and sheds less) rather than fur (which has a shorter life cycle and sheds frequently).

Individual Variation: A person may react strongly to one Poodle but not another. Experts recommend spending time with a specific dog before adoption to test your personal tolerance.
